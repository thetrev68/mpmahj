# Strategy

## General Strategy Pointers for Beginners

Listen closely, young one, for these lessons will serve you well on your journey to mastering American Mahjong.

### Do Not Rush to Meld

Many beginners believe that making many melds—Chows, Pungs, Kongs—is the mark of success. This is a misunderstanding. Melding is a burden, not a triumph! Watch experienced players and you will see they do not seize every Pung opportunity that arises. There are good reasons for this:

- When you expose a meld, you reveal three tiles to your opponents. They can learn far more about your hand from your melds and discards than from discards alone.
- Melding narrows the possibilities for the hand you are building. You will understand this quickly as you play.

In general, do not claim another player's discard unless you have a clear plan for your hand, and that specific discard brings you meaningfully closer to victory.

### Keep Your Pairs

It is harder to complete a pair when you hold only one tile than it is to complete a Pung when you hold a pair. Therefore, if you have a pair, do not be too quick to claim a matching tile to form a Pung.

### Exercise Patience

When first learning, it is common to grab every opportunity to meld a Pung or Chow. In the early stages of the game, remember that many good tiles remain available for drawing from the Wall. By not melding your tiles, you keep your intentions hidden from others, and you give yourself the chance to achieve a Concealed Hand.

### Remain Flexible

As you build your hand, be prepared to abandon your earlier plans when you observe what tiles others are discarding. If you are playing Western Mah-Jongg with restrictions on winning hands, do not be too quick to form your only Chow—other opportunities will arise.

### Prevent Others from Winning

As much as you desire victory yourself, sometimes it is wiser to prevent anyone else from winning. This is especially true when you might "feed" a high-scoring hand. If a player has melded three sets of all one suit, this is particularly dangerous—you might feed a Pure or Clean hand and pay a high price. This is why players announce the danger when making a third meld in one suit.

### Watch the Discards and the Wall

Observe the discards carefully and watch the number of tiles remaining in the Wall. As the game approaches its end, tension increases, and it becomes more important to be cautious with your discards when fewer tiles remain to be drawn. If the Wall is growing short, do not discard any tiles you do not see among the discards already played.

## American Strategy (NMJL)

As stated above, there is usually no single "best" or "right" strategy for any particular situation. Strategies must be adjusted according to the circumstances—considering the probabilities, the other players, the length of the wall, how fortunate you feel, and more. The skilled player always employs a flexible strategic approach.

### Organize Your Tiles

As soon as you receive your initial deal, sort your tiles on the sloping front of your rack. Arrange flowers at the left, then by suit (numerically within each suit), then dragons and winds and jokers. Suits are extremely important, so you must always separate tiles by suit. The order of suits (which suit at the left, which at the right) is not important at this stage.

### Identify Your Hand

Once your tiles are sorted, look for pairs and triples first, and see if they suggest a particular section of the card. If not, see if any of the singles do. If you have ones and eights and nines, you might pursue a section featuring those numbers. Look at your winds, dragons, and flowers—do they suggest any particular section of the card? Most players do not find uses for winds in particular, and you probably will not either, but consider what if other players were to pass you winds during the Charleston? Do not spend too much time thinking—the others wish to proceed to the Charleston.

### Charleston Strategy

1. During the first Charleston, pass tiles that do not help you make a hand in the section of the card you have targeted. Observe whether the tiles passed to you do help.

2. During the second Charleston, you will most likely see mostly the same tiles you saw in the first one. Consider whether you should change your hand based on the tiles being passed. Consider also whether you ought to stop the Charleston.

3. Do not be one who stops the Charleston too frequently—this can anger other players.

4. Do not be too quick to take a discard in the early part of the game. Most likely there will be more opportunities later. You do not wish to reveal your hand too early. Sometimes a single meld will tell everyone very clearly exactly what hand you are targeting!

5. Save your jokers for later in the game. Early melding of jokers merely allows others to redeem them—you do not wish to assist your opponents, do you?

6. As you near the end of the hand (as the wall grows short), watch the discards carefully—you do not wish to discard any "raw tiles" (tiles not present among the discards) and give someone the victory. You pay double when you give someone the win.

7. Try to keep your hand concealed. Showing completed sets provides valuable information to your opponents, who can use that information to thwart your efforts.

8. Pursue the high-scoring hands. You will not succeed most of the time, but you need only achieve a few good scores to be the big winner! Lose small and win big.

9. When you reach the point where you can see that you cannot make your hand, you cannot declare yourself dead. However, you may discard any tile from your hand that you believe is safe, while you focus on preventing others from winning. Breaking up your hand is called "dogging" the hand. If you maintain a poker face—meaning you do not reveal what you are doing through words or body language—perhaps no one will even know what you are doing. But do not discard the safest tiles first; save those silver bullets for last. Jokers, for instance. Jokers are the safest discard in the deck, because jokers die the instant they are discarded, and no one can use them.

10. As mentioned previously, jokers are safe to discard, since no one can ever claim a discarded joker. Sometimes it is necessary, when you cannot use it because you need to complete a pair or fill a single. When discarding a joker, many people say "same." It is encouraged to say "same" because the practice encourages other players to keep their eyes open, not only their ears. You can even simply say the name of the previous tile (you do not even have to say "same"). There is a strategic reason for not merely saying "joker" when discarding a joker. An opponent who foolishly listens only, and does not look, will probably miss the fact that you have discarded a joker, and will not even know it until she happens to see it on the discard floor later. Or when she hears you say "same," she might think you had discarded a joker and will scan the table looking for it, perhaps even asking what just happened. Either way, by saying "same," you have taken advantage of some players' poor habits.

11. Consecutive Runs hand #2 is perennially the easiest hand on the card to make. Consider: the easiest section on the card is Consecutive Runs, for a simple reason: this section is based on consecutive numbers, and number tiles are the most numerous type of tile in the mah-jongg set. Consecutive #2 is the easiest hand in Consecutive Runs to make, because it needs only two suits, it has no pairs (meaning you can expose, and use jokers in, any grouping in the hand), and you can use any four consecutive numbers to make it. It is very flexible—meaning you can switch within it, or switch to it, if necessary—and it is easy to fool opponents with ambiguous exposures. Consecutive #2 is "the most powerful hand on the card."

## Additional American Strategy

1. After someone exposes a pung or kong, study the card carefully and see which hands are possibilities. If it is early in the game, discard tiles which you think they may be able to use but probably cannot call because they have either used their only joker on the first exposure or have not collected enough tiles to make a second exposure. However, discard to a second exposure at great risk and never discard to a third exposure, even if you must break up a good hand.

2. During the Charleston, pay attention to which tiles do not come back to you. Try to determine, without being too obvious, which tiles the recipient keeps.

3. If you play with the same people regularly, try to determine their playing style. Are they cautious, always playing "easy" hands? Do they take risks?

4. Try to find one hand and stick with it. The best advice you can receive is: "You cannot play everything."

5. Study the card at a time when you are not playing. Knowing which direction to pursue in the moment can give you an advantage.

6. Redeem a joker from someone else's hand even if you do not need it. You can always discard it and prevent someone else from obtaining it.

7. Remember that the pairs are the most difficult to obtain since they cannot be exposed. It is better to play a hand where the pairs are in place and you need a kong or pung rather than the reverse.
